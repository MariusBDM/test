A Comprehensive Guide to Road Safety

Being a fully alert driver is one of the most important factors in preventing accidents. Learn how to stay safe on the road and prepare for your theory test.

Checking for Hazards and Making Turns

Before making any move on the road—such as a U-turn—always check for oncoming traffic by looking over your shoulder. This simple but crucial step can prevent accidents, particularly at intersections or blind spots where visibility may be limited. Remember to slow down when approaching areas with limited visibility, like bends or bridges, and avoid overtaking in risky spots. For more guidance on handling hazards, visit the THINK! Road Safety website.

Dealing with Distractions

Never use a hand-held phone while driving. If the light has been green for a while, be prepared for it to turn red. Learn more in our Distraction Awareness Guide.

Visibility and Safety Precautions

Maintaining clear visibility is essential for safe driving. If the road ahead isn’t clear, reduce your speed to give yourself more reaction time. Ensure that your windshield is free of stickers or objects that could obstruct your view. Signal early when changing lanes or making turns to inform other road users of your intentions. For more on maintaining visibility, read our Visibility Tips.

Managing Fatigue and Poor Driving Conditions

If you feel tired, stop and take a break—fatigue can be fatal. Driving in wet conditions requires extra caution—keep both hands steady on the wheel and drive cautiously. When starting off from a stop, always check your blind spots by looking over your shoulder, especially when merging into traffic. Stay safe during poor weather conditions with our guide on driving in poor conditions.

Handling Roads with Obstacles

When driving on highways or one-way streets, always look ahead for potential obstacles, such as windshield pillars that could obstruct your view. These can block your line of sight, making it difficult to see pedestrians or cyclists. If you’re reversing from a driveway, ask someone to help guide you if your visibility is limited.

Regular Mirror Checks and Avoiding Blind Spots

Checking your mirrors frequently is crucial, but mirrors don’t cover everything—blind spots still exist. For complex maneuvers like reversing in tight spaces or parking, ask for assistance to minimize the risk of accidents.

Using Satnav and Other Technology Safely

While satnavs are helpful for navigation, they can also be distracting if not set up properly. Always set your satnav before starting your journey to avoid unnecessary distractions while driving. Once on the road, focus on driving and let the satnav guide you. Learn more about safely using tech with our Technology and Safe Driving Guide.

  • Never use a hand-held mobile phone while driving except in emergencies.
  • Do not accelerate at long-standing green traffic lights—prepare for them to change.
  • Avoid staying too close to large vehicles—you’ll be in their blind spot.
  • Do not ignore obstructions like stickers or hanging objects that block your view.
  • Do not drive when tired—take breaks when needed.
  • Never ignore blind spots—always check over your shoulder.
  • Do not use your satnav or phone while driving—set it up before you start.
  • Do not reverse without ensuring you have clear visibility or assistance when needed.
  • Do not forget to use signals early—inform other drivers of your intentions in advance.
  • Do not overtake in areas with poor visibility, such as after bends or near junctions.
  • Do not get distracted by windscreen pillars—they can block your view of pedestrians and cyclists.
  • Never assume it's safe without checking mirrors—mirrors can have blind spots.
  • Do not assume the road will stay clear after a bend—visibility and conditions may change rapidly.
